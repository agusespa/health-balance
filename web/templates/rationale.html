How it works

Biological Reserve Score (BRS)

A quantitative model estimating how much long-term health reserve you are building to stay capable and resilient as you age.

The Master Algorithm

The score is designed around aging. It combines an Aging Tax, which reflects the rising cost of maintaining reserve over time, Reserve Markers, which reflect your current long-term capacity, and Reserve-Building Behaviors, which help build and protect that capacity over time.

The Aging Tax

$$\text{Tax} = \text{Score} \times \frac{(\text{Age}^2 / 8000)}{52}$$

This formula reflects the accelerating nature of biological decay. Unlike linear models, a quadratic approach keeps the cost of maintaining reserve relatively low in youth but makes it rise more meaningfully with age.

The idea is that the same level of reserve is harder to preserve later in life. That keeps aging central to the model instead of treating the score as a simple sum of healthy habits.

The Reserve Backbone

Metrics such as VO2 Max, Waist-to-Height Ratio, Resting Heart Rate, and selected cognitive signals form the backbone of the score. They carry the most weight because they are the closest available signals of the reserve you are likely to carry into older age.

These measures should move slowly and anchor the score in underlying capacity, so the result is not overly driven by one good or bad week.

The Supporting Layer

Behaviors such as workouts, sleep, nutrition, mobility, steps, mindfulness, and learning still matter because they are some of the most important ways to build and protect reserve over time.

The goal is to reward consistency toward strong targets. These behaviors should meaningfully influence the score, but they should not let one unusually active week create an unrealistic jump in long-term reserve.

Basic Health Pillar

Measuring recovery quality and cardiometabolic risk.

  • Sleep Quality: Uses a baseline of 75 as a useful reference point. Consistent, high-quality sleep supports recovery, hormone balance, and the ability to preserve reserve over time.
  • Structural Ratio (WHtR): A core reserve marker. Maintaining a Waist-to-Height Ratio below 0.48 is a strong sign of lower metabolic risk and better odds of aging with less disease burden.
  • Resting Heart Rate (RHR): A long-term cardiovascular and recovery signal. Sustained increases can point to stress, illness, deconditioning, or poor recovery.
  • Nutrition Quality: A self-reported 1-10 index. Scores above 7 represent an anti-inflammatory diet, fueling the Biological Reserve rather than depleting it.

Fitness Pillar

Measuring physical capacity and the behaviors that help preserve it into older age.

  • VO2 Max: One of the most important reserve markers in the model. As a strong predictor of healthy aging, it represents how much aerobic capacity you are carrying into later life.
  • Cardio Recovery: Measures the 60s heart rate drop after vigorous effort (70%+ max HR). A drop of 25+ BPM indicates a robust "parasympathetic brake," a key marker of resilience and recovery quality.
  • Supporting Behaviors: Steps, workouts, and mobility are included because they are some of the best ways to build and maintain reserve.

Cognition Pillar

Measuring cognitive reserve and the habits that help protect it.

  • Mindfulness: Active cortisol management. Regular practice protects the brain from the neurotoxic effects of chronic stress and supports long-term recovery and regulation.
  • Cognitive Novelty: Measures time spent in "Deep Learning" states. Learning complex new symbolic systems (music notation/foreign syntax) builds Cognitive Reserve, creating a buffer against age-related decline when practiced consistently.
  • Working Memory (Dual N-Back): A useful supporting signal for cognitive capacity. It can help reflect training and adaptation, but it should not outweigh the major physical reserve markers.
  • Reaction Speed: Identifies acute neurological fatigue and longer-term slowing. Tracked over time, it can help detect changes in processing speed and resilience.

Disclaimer

The Biological Reserve Score (BRS) is a motivational tool designed for general health tracking and lifestyle optimization. It is a mathematical model, not a medical diagnostic. These metrics do not replace professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always consult with a healthcare provider before making significant changes to your exercise or nutrition routine.
